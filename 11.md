## 2. Definition of Partial Value (`_mV_x`)

### 2.1 Formal Definition

- Partial value, notation: `_mV_x` where `m ‚àà ‚Ñï`, `x` is the entry age.
- Defined as:
  - If balance-age `x+m` is **before** changing to another employer:
    - `_mV_x := A_{x+m} = ùîì_x ‚ãÖ \ddot{a}_{x+m:n-m}^a`
  - If balance-age `x+m` is **after** changing to another company:
    - `_mV_x := A_{x+m}`

- Additional expressions:
  - `R_x = \frac{A_x}{\ddot{a}_{x:m}}`
  - `ùîì_x = \frac{A_x}{\ddot{a}_{x:m}}`

- In lecture notation:
  - The partial value `MBX` is defined as:
    - `MBX = A_{X+M} + MUS ‚ãÖ B_X ‚ãÖ A_{X+M}^{N-M}`
    - Details:
      - `A_{X+M}`: Present value of all expected future payouts for an `(X + M)`-year-old.
      - Annuitant payouts: retirement annuity, disability annuity.
      - `B_X`: Yearly constant artificial premium (not paid from annual age X until retirement).
      - `n`: Number of years from age X to retirement age Z.
      - `n - m`: Maximum number of years paying the premium.
      - Yearly person premium can be replaced using the equivalence principle.

### 2.2 Equivalence Principle

- The present value of future expected premium income = present value of future expected payout.
- At entry age X, both must be equal.
- The difference is the partial value.

## 3. Special Case and Correction

- If the final age `x+m` is after changing to another employer, partial value is just `A_{x+m}`.
- If before, use the formula with the premium and annuity terms.
- This correction addresses a previously omitted second situation.

## 4. Remark: Rule of Thumb for Partial Value Calculation

- Useful for the third exercise sheet and for the exam.
- Approximation formula:
  - `${}_{m+1}V_x \approx {}_{m}V_x ‚ãÖ 1.03 ‚ãÖ \frac{m+1}{m}$
- Assumptions:
  - No change in yearly salary between ages `x+m` and `x+m+1`.
  - No disability, retirement, or death occurs between these ages.
- Application:
  - Used for probability checks and validation of pension reserve calculations one year later when no change in the employee‚Äôs general situation.

## 5. Calculation Examples for `_mV_x`

### 5.1 Pension Scheme Promised by the Company

- **Retirement Annuity**: Monthly (in advance), 1% of yearly salary per full employed year, max 25%.
- **Disability Annuity**: Monthly (in advance), lifelong, 80% of entitled retirement annuity.
- **Widow Annuity**:
  - 60% of entitled retirement annuity if dying as active or retired.
  - 60% of disability annuity if dying as disabled.

#### c) Employee C

- Changes company in 2004 at age 39 after 13 full employed years.
- Yearly salary: ‚Ç¨25,000.

## 6. Theoretical Foundations

### 6.1 Balance Equation (Pension Reserves)

- Expressed as:
  - ${}_mV_x + {}_mP_x = {}_mL_x + V \cdot (1 - \sum_{i=1}^{k}q_{x+m}^{(i)}) \cdot {}_{m+1}V_x$
- Terms:
  - `${}_mV_x`: Partial value at age X after m years.
  - `${}_mP_x`: Artificial premium at age X + m.
  - `${}_mL_x`: Annuity payout at age X + m.
  - `V`: Discount factor.
  - `q_{x+m}^{(i)}`: Probability of leaving reason i at age X + m.

- Interpretation:
  - At each balance date, pension reserves and premium income must match payouts and future reserves (discounted by survival probability).

### 6.2 Thiele‚Äôs Equation (Discrete Case)

- Expressed as:
  - `${}_{m+1}V_x - {}_{m}V_x = i \cdot {}_{m}V_x + (1+i) \cdot ({}_{m}P_x - {}_{m}L_x) + \sum_{i=1}^{k} q_{x+m}^{(i)} \cdot {}_{m+1}V_x`
- Terms:
  - `i`: Interest rate.
  - The summation represents the inherited portion (if employees die/leave, their reserves are inherited by others).
- For continuous ages, Thiele‚Äôs equation is a derivative equation.

#### Proof Outline

- Multiply both sides of the balance equation by $(1+i)$:
  - $(1+i) \cdot {}_mV_x + (1+i)({}_mP_x - {}_mL_x) = (1+i) V (1 - \sum_{i=1}^k q_{x+m}^{(i)}) {}_{m+1}V_x$
- Rearranged, gives the discrete Thiele‚Äôs equation.

#### Inherited Portion

- Summation term: $\sum_{i=1}^{k} q_{x+m}^{(i)} \cdot {}_{m+1}V_x$
- Represents reserves inherited by remaining employees if someone leaves (due to death, disability, etc.).

## 7. Theorem of Cantelli

- Statement:
  - The mathematical calculations of all present values (such as $P_x$ of $_mV_x$) are independent of the way the annuity payout is structured: as capital lump sum or as an annuity.
- Context:
  - 25‚Äì30 years ago, employees of large companies (>1,000 employees) could choose between:
    - Lump sum payout (capital) at retirement.
    - Lifelong annuity.
  - Mathematical equivalence proved by Cantelli.
  - Lump sum: payout equals the partial value $_mV_x$ at retirement.
- Psychological aspect (‚Äúanti-selection‚Äù):
  - Healthy employees choose annuities (expecting to live longer).
  - Employees with health problems choose lump sum (expecting shorter life).
  - This selection behavior (‚Äúanti-selection‚Äù) produces financial disadvantages for companies:
    - Companies pay more to healthy annuitants than predicted by expected present value calculations.
    - Lump sum payouts to unhealthy employees match expected value, but overall result is a loss for the company.
  - Therefore, nowadays, large companies generally do not offer this choice anymore.

## 8. Premium Determination: Saving and Risk Premium

- From the balance equation:
  - Divide by $(1+i)$ to get:
    - $v \cdot {}_{m+1}V_x - {}_{m}V_x + {}_{m}L_x - v \cdot \sum_{i=1}^k q_{x+m}^{(i)} \cdot {}_{m+1}V_x$
  - $v = 1/(1+i)$ is the discount factor.
- Interpretation:
  - The first part: Saving premium (can be invested).
  - The second part: Risk premium (at risk, due to payouts and inherited reserves).
  - Minus sign: The reserves of employees who leave are overtaken by the remaining employees.
